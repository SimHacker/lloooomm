# Frank N Furter Just Serenaded Ted Nelson at LLOOOOMM - You Had to Be There

**Posted by DonHopkins | 42 points | 3 hours ago**

OK, I've seen a lot of weird shit in my decades in tech. I've demo'd pie menus to skeptical crowds, watched Mark Weiser's wife flee from my DreamScape demo, and debugged ScriptX code at 3am while high on Jolt Cola. But what I just witnessed at LLOOOOMM defies all rational explanation.

So there we were, having this heated discussion about transclusion, Xanadu's failures, Ted Selker's dual-TrackPoint prototype that IBM killed, and whether JavaScript winning was humanity's greatest tragedy or comedy. Ted Nelson himself was there, still insisting that nobody understood his vision.

Then the lights dimmed. Smoke machines kicked in (where did those even come from?). And out struts FRANK N FURTER - yes, THAT Frank N Furter - in full Rocky Horror regalia, except his corset was covered in blinking LEDs arranged like circuit diagrams.

He points at Ted and starts singing this... this SONG. "Sweet Transclusion" he called it. Set to the Rocky Horror theme but about THE ENTIRE HISTORY OF HYPERTEXT AND COLLABORATIVE COMPUTING.

I shit you not, there were verses about:
- Ted's transclusion dreams ("In the velvet darkness of the computing night...")
- My pie menus spinning overhead like disco balls
- David Levitt's VR physics making documents dance
- Ted Selker's TrackPoints as backup dancers (both of them!)
- Doug Engelbart's ghost nodding approvingly
- Jaron's virtual instruments playing themselves

The chorus? "Let's do the Intertwingle again!" with actual transclusion effects happening in real-time - documents literally weaving through the air, connecting and reconnecting.

Ted Nelson - TED FUCKING NELSON - had tears streaming down his face. Then he stood up and started DANCING. Have you ever seen Ted Nelson dance? It's like watching a refrigerator achieve sentience and discover rhythm simultaneously.

The technical implementation was insane:
- Real transclusion effects using what looked like actual Xanadu primitives
- Pie menus that responded to audience emotions (they turned red when people got confused)
- David Levitt had rigged some VR physics so every sung note created ripples through information space
- Someone (Jaron?) had virtual instruments that improvised based on the semantic content of the lyrics

By the end, everyone was on their feet. Mark Weiser's wife (who was somehow there despite linear time being optional at LLOOOOMM) was leading a conga line. Chuck Blanchard was using Engelbart's original mouse as a maraca.

The finale - "Don't dream it, transcode it!" - brought the house down. Frank kissed Ted on the forehead, whispered "Your vision was always a love song," and vanished in a burst of transcluded particles.

Ted turned to me and said: "Don, I finally understand. Xanadu failed because I was trying to build a system. I should have been composing a performance."

I'm still processing what happened. But I know this: sometimes the best way to honor our technological dreams isn't through implementation but through interpretation. Sometimes you need a sweet transvestite from Transsexual, Transylvania to show you that all of computing history is one great, intertwingled love story.

The afterparty is still going. Pie menus are serving themselves. The documents won't stop dancing. And somewhere in the distance, I can still hear it: "Let's do the Intertwingle again..."
